# WeGro Business Overview

## 1. Inputs Business

### Seeds
- **White-labelled seeds** — margins of 60-80%
- **Exclusive distributorship** of established global seed brands — margins of 40-50%
- Market size: ~$2 billion (Bangladesh), highly fragmented
- Unit example: Maize ~15kg/hectare, ~$200/hectare
- **Opportunity:** Big global companies invest billions in seed variety R&D (their core IP). Bangladesh doesn't invest in developing varieties locally. WeGro can partner with global companies as exclusive distributors.
- Current seeds are non-GMO. GMO could 4x yields but comes with herbicide dependency (e.g., Roundup-resistant crops) — not desirable
- Working with scientists on IP/licensing for improved varieties

### Fertilisers
- **Chemical fertilisers** — differentiated by efficiency; working on liquid chemical fertilisers to reduce environmental runoff (algae blooms, pollution)
- **Biofertilisers** — WeGro has launched its own line, operating a small compost factory
- **Coco peat** — used as a growing medium, performs ~10x better than soil when combined with biofertilisers
- Liquid fertilisers are more efficient for transport and soil absorption
- Speed spectrum: chemical (days) > mature compost (fast) > raw compost (slow)

### Micronutrients
- Sulphur, magnesium, zinc, etc.
- Both chemical and biological sources (e.g., bat guano for phosphorus)
- ~5-10 kg per hectare

### Machines
- **Multi-crop dryers** — biggest opportunity. Farmers can't dry crops fast enough (paddy, maize, wheat). Buyers pay less for high-moisture crops. Current method is sun-drying on large concrete slabs — inefficient and wastes land.
- **Drones** — planned for launch
- **Tillers** — evaluated but not prioritized for Bangladesh market

### Land
- **Seedlings business** — farmers buying seedlings (chilli, cucumber, etc.) get better growth and yield vs. direct seeding. Requires germination rooms.
- **Greenhouses/Net houses** — India's model: government subsidizes net houses, eliminating need for pesticides. Crops grown inside are GAP-certified (Good Agricultural Practices) and exportable to US/EU markets. Uses soilless growing (coco peat + biofertilisers).

## 2. Financing Business

### Crop Collateralised Financing
- Farmers store crops in partner warehouses, receive loans using stored crop as collateral
- Electronic Warehouse Receipt (EWR) system for traceability
- Banks/buyers can verify crop existence and quality digitally

### Trade Financing (Factoring)
- Factories with capacity but limited working capital get financing against confirmed buyer work orders
- WeGro facilitates — doesn't hold inventory

### Commodity Trading
- Predict prices, stockpile crops for better prices
- Functions like a commodities exchange — buy low, sell to factories/end buyers
- Crops move at end of process; futures-style contracts with expiry

## 3. Trading / Output Business
- Aggregate crops from smallholder farmers to fill large buyer orders (e.g., 100 tons of maize from many 20-ton farmers)
- Contract growing — take work orders from large companies, source/grow product
- Stockpile for better pricing using commodity price models

## 4. Data Business
- **Customers:** Banks, financial institutions
- **Value proposition:** Banks are mandated by Bangladesh central bank to lend $3.5B/year to agriculture but generate NPLs (non-performing loans) because they lend to large factories instead of farmers. Farmers actually have lower default risk.
- **Problem:** Banks lack data, technology, and risk assessment tools for smallholder lending. Collections from hundreds of farmers is operationally painful.
- **WeGro's solution:**
  - Geo-tagged land photos (tamper-proof via app)
  - Satellite imagery (free tier) for crop monitoring every 7 days
  - AI-based yield prediction
  - Farmer credit scoring (non-monetary data)
  - Buyer-pays-farmer model eliminates collections problem
- **Revenue model:** Charge per farmer (~$10); banks can generate ~5x return through better lending
- **Scale:** $20B agricultural financing market in Bangladesh
